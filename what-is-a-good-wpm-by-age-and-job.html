What Is a Good WPM by Age and Job?

When you take a typing test, it’s natural to wonder: “Is my WPM actually good?” The answer depends a lot on your age and what you use typing for: school, office work, programming, gaming, or professional writing.

In this guide, we’ll break down realistic typing speed benchmarks by age and by job, so you can see where you stand and what to aim for. We’ll also cover why accuracy matters more than raw speed and how to improve your WPM without burning out.

If you’re not sure how WPM is calculated, you can read our full introduction in What Is WPM and How Do We Measure Typing Speed?

Quick Overview: What Counts as a Good Typing Speed?

For most people, these ranges are a useful starting point:

  • Under 20 WPM – Beginner, still learning the keyboard
  • 20–40 WPM – Basic everyday typing
  • 40–60 WPM – Typical average adult typist
  • 60–80 WPM – Fast and efficient for most jobs
  • 80–100 WPM – Advanced, very comfortable typist
  • 100+ WPM – Expert / competitive level

Remember: these are guidelines, not strict rules. A “good” speed is whatever lets you work comfortably and accurately.

Good WPM by Job or Use Case

Different jobs demand different typing speeds. A novelist, a developer, and a casual email user don’t need the same WPM to be effective. Below you’ll find suggested speed ranges for common roles.

Students (School, College, University)

Students type essays, notes, research summaries, and online messages. A comfortable typing speed makes studying less stressful and exam time more manageable.

  • Target 40–60 WPM with at least 95% accuracy
  • Above 60 WPM is a bonus, especially for timed exams and note-taking

Office Workers & Administrative Roles

Office workers send emails, write reports, and use business software all day. Typing is often a quiet “background” skill that has a huge impact on productivity.

  • Comfortable 50–70 WPM
  • Accuracy is crucial – fewer corrections saves time over the whole day
  • Keyboard shortcuts (Ctrl+C/V, Alt+Tab, etc.) can matter as much as raw WPM

Programmers & Developers

Developers don’t just type plain text – they type code, symbols, and commands. Thinking, debugging, and reading code are often slower than typing.

  • Effective range 50–70 WPM
  • Higher speeds help, but clarity and accuracy in code matter more
  • Comfort with shortcuts and editors (VS Code, IDEs) boosts overall efficiency

Writers, Bloggers & Journalists

Professional writers spend a lot of time at the keyboard. Good WPM doesn’t replace creativity, but it does make getting ideas out of your head much easier.

  • Productive 60–80 WPM
  • Many experienced writers naturally reach 70+ WPM over time
  • Editing and rewriting still take a large share of the time

Data Entry, Transcription & Typing-Heavy Jobs

These roles rely heavily on speed and precision. Here, WPM is often part of the job requirement.

  • Minimum 60 WPM with high accuracy
  • Competitive 70–90+ WPM
  • Mistakes can be costly, so accuracy above 97–98% is usually expected

Gamers & Esports Players

Some games, especially MMOs and strategy titles, involve rapid chatting and commands. Others rely more on mouse and controller skills than keyboard speed.

  • Useful range 40–60 WPM
  • For competitive play and fast in-game communication, 60+ WPM helps

These ranges are suggestions, not strict rules. A person at 45 WPM with almost no errors can easily outperform a 70 WPM typist who constantly needs to correct mistakes.

How to Measure Your WPM Correctly

Before you compare yourself to any chart, it’s important to measure your typing speed in a consistent way.

  • Use a test that lasts at least 1 minute
  • Type natural text, not just repeated letters
  • Check if the test shows both WPM and accuracy
  • Repeat the test a few times and take your average

WPM vs Accuracy: Which Matters More?

High WPM with low accuracy is like driving fast but missing every turn. For work, school, and everyday life, you’ll get better results by aiming for:

  • WPM that feels comfortable (no stress, no pain)
  • Accuracy of at least 95%
  • Good posture and relaxed hands

If your WPM is slightly below the “average” range but your accuracy is very high, you’re on a solid path. Speed will increase naturally as your fingers become more confident. Our guide Typing Accuracy Tips focuses specifically on reducing errors.

What If I’m Below These WPM Ranges?

Don’t worry – benchmarks are not a test you fail. They’re just reference points. Many people start at 15–25 WPM and slowly climb up with the right habits.

Simple Plan to Improve Your WPM

  • Practice 10–15 minutes a day instead of once a week
  • Use touch typing technique and home row positioning
  • Spend a few minutes on slow accuracy drills
  • Then add short speed bursts (1-minute tests)
  • Track your progress each week, not each single session

Final Thoughts: “Good” WPM Is Personal

A good typing speed is not a fixed magic number like 60 or 100 WPM. It’s the point where you can type your thoughts, work tasks, or messages comfortably, accurately, and without constant corrections.

Use these age and job benchmarks as a guide, not a judgment. If you’re already within the suggested range for your situation – great. If not, consistent practice can get you there sooner than you think.
