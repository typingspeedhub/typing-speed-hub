Average Typing Speed by Age

Typing speed usually improves with regular keyboard time rather than age itself. Teens who game or type daily can be faster than adults who type occasionally. The biggest separator is typically habit + technique (touch typing, posture, accuracy control).

Age Group Average WPM (Typical) Good WPM (Comfortable) Notes
10–14 25–35 WPM 40+ WPM Rapid gains possible with short daily practice.
15–24 35–45 WPM 55+ WPM Often improves quickly with games, school work, or coding.
25–44 40–50 WPM 60+ WPM Consistency matters most; work demands can raise daily volume.
45+ 35–45 WPM 55+ WPM Comfort + ergonomics can matter more than pushing speed.

If your accuracy is below ~95%, start with control and rhythm. See: typing tips.

Typing Speed for Office Work

Recommended WPM: 45–60+ (with high accuracy)

Office jobs involve emails, reports, spreadsheets, CRM notes, chats, and meeting follow-ups. Most tasks reward consistent speed rather than “test-speed.” You’ll type a lot of short bursts, often while switching between tabs and tools.

What matters most in office roles

  • Accuracy: fewer corrections and less editing time
  • Consistency: steady pace across many short tasks
  • Comfort: lower fatigue across a full workday
  • Formatting confidence: numbers, punctuation, and basic shortcuts

Realistic office targets

  • Entry-level: 40–50 WPM can be enough
  • Comfortable professional: 50–60 WPM
  • Heavy typing/admin roles: 60+ WPM helps

If you type daily for work, a short routine like the 10-minute typing drill is usually the fastest way to improve.

In practice, going from 40 → 55 WPM often feels like a superpower: emails are faster, meeting notes are less stressful, and you spend less time fixing typos. Beyond ~70 WPM, gains are smaller unless your role is very typing-heavy.

Typing Speed for Programmers (Coding)

Recommended WPM: 45–70 (but accuracy and symbols matter more)

Programmers don’t “type all day” in the same way writers do. Coding includes thinking time, debugging, reading, and short bursts of typing with symbols and structure. That means raw WPM isn’t the whole story.

Key coding skills that beat pure speed

  • Low typo rate: fewer broken builds and wasted minutes
  • Comfort with symbols: parentheses, braces, quotes, semicolons
  • Keyboard shortcuts: navigation, selection, multi-cursor, search
  • Touch typing: staying in “flow” without looking down

If you’re at 45–55 WPM with good accuracy, you’re already in a solid zone for coding. If you want a meaningful upgrade, focus on punctuation accuracy and rhythm. The technique basics in Typing Tips often improve coding comfort quickly.

Typing Speed for Writers & Content Creators

Recommended WPM: 55–80+ (for heavy writing days)

Writers benefit from higher typing speed because output volume can be large: drafting articles, newsletters, scripts, reports, or books. But writing is also thinking. What matters most is staying in flow and reducing friction.

Writer’s shortcut: Instead of chasing peak WPM, aim for a higher comfortable average. Consistent 65 WPM with accuracy beats a shaky 85 WPM that forces editing every sentence.

Why speed helps writers

  • Captures ideas before they disappear
  • Reduces “stop-start” editing interruptions
  • Helps drafting sessions feel smoother and more enjoyable

Typing Speed for Students & School Work

Recommended WPM: 40–60

Students write essays, take notes, answer timed questions, and communicate digitally. A comfortable typing speed reduces stress during deadlines and online exams. It also frees mental energy for the actual thinking part.

Student-friendly targets

  • Middle school: 30–40 WPM is a solid start
  • High school: 40–55 WPM for essays and projects
  • College: 50–60 WPM helps with long papers and fast note-taking
The easiest routine to stick with: the 10-minute typing drill. It’s short enough to do daily and long enough to improve.

Extra Job Benchmarks: Customer Support, Data Entry, Transcription, and More

Not all “office jobs” are the same. Some roles require short bursts of typing, while others involve constant keyboard input. Here are realistic ranges for common job categories, plus what matters most in each one.

Job Type Good WPM Range What Matters Most Why
Customer Support / Chat Support 50–70 WPM Accuracy, speed consistency, shortcuts Fast, clear replies and fewer corrections improve customer experience.
Data Entry 55–80 WPM (or KPH metrics) Numeric accuracy, rhythm, low error rate One typo can cause downstream issues; accuracy often beats raw speed.
Transcription (general) 60–90 WPM Accuracy, endurance, punctuation Long sessions require stamina; clean text reduces editing time.
Medical / Legal Transcription 70–100+ WPM Precision, terminology, formatting Specialized vocabulary and high stakes push accuracy requirements up.
Journalist / Reporter 55–85 WPM Speed under pressure, accuracy, quotes Fast notes and drafts help during deadlines and live events.
Administrative Assistant 50–70 WPM Consistency, formatting, fewer mistakes Emails, documents, scheduling notes—lots of small tasks all day.
Student (timed essays/exams) 45–65 WPM Accuracy, comfort, low stress Typing should not be the bottleneck in timed writing.
Software Engineer 45–70 WPM Symbols, shortcuts, low typos Coding is “think + type”; reliability beats peak speed.

Quick tip: if your role involves a lot of typing, improving from “okay” to “comfortable” is usually a 2–6 week project with short daily practice. Start here: Typing Speed Test.

What if your job uses KPH instead of WPM?

Some employers (especially for data entry) use KPH (keystrokes per hour) or numeric entry tests. The principle is the same: accuracy first, then speed. If you notice errors on numbers, dates, or IDs, slow down slightly and focus on clean inputs. A consistent workflow will outperform sporadic “fast but messy” bursts.

How Much Time Faster Typing Really Saves (Realistic Examples)

Faster typing isn’t just a bragging number. It can save real time—especially if you type a lot for work or school. Here’s an easy way to think about it: speed gains matter most when you type many minutes per day and when your accuracy stays high.

Example A: Office emails and notes

Imagine you type the equivalent of 20 minutes of continuous text per workday (emails, notes, docs). That’s not constant typing—just the total “hands on keyboard” writing time.

  • 40 WPM: ~800 words in 20 minutes
  • 60 WPM: ~1200 words in 20 minutes

Difference: you can finish the same writing workload with less pressure, or produce more in the same time.

Example B: Student essays

A 1200-word essay draft (not counting research) is easier when typing isn’t the bottleneck.

  • 40 WPM: about 30 minutes of pure typing time
  • 60 WPM: about 20 minutes of pure typing time

The bigger win is mental: faster typing helps you keep ideas flowing without constant pauses.

The “hidden cost”: error correction

If your accuracy drops, time savings disappear. Backspacing, retyping words, and fixing punctuation can easily add minutes back. That’s why most professionals should prioritize high-accuracy speed: aim for 95–97%+ accuracy, then gradually push pace. If you want a clean routine, use the 10-minute typing drill.

Rule of thumb: A “good WPM” is the speed you can maintain with high accuracy when you’re tired, distracted, and multitasking. That’s the speed you’ll actually use at work.

Is Your Typing Speed Holding You Back?

Many people worry they’re “too slow” for a job. In reality, the question is more specific: Is typing limiting your performance in the tasks you do most?

Signs typing speed is a bottleneck

  • You avoid writing long messages because it feels slow.
  • You lose your train of thought while typing.
  • You struggle in timed tests/exams.
  • You constantly look down at the keyboard.
  • You make frequent typos and spend time fixing them.

Signs it’s NOT the main problem

  • You type comfortably for your daily workload.
  • Your errors are rare and easy to spot.
  • You finish tasks on time, even if you’re not “fast.”
  • The job is mostly thinking/analysis with short typing bursts.

A simple self-test

Do this once:

  1. Take a baseline on our Typing Speed Test.
  2. Note your WPM and accuracy.
  3. Then ask: “Do I feel limited by typing in real life?”

If you’re under ~40 WPM and you do a lot of writing, improving to 50 WPM can noticeably reduce stress. If you’re already 50–60 WPM with good accuracy, the biggest improvements often come from shortcuts, organization, and clarity—not just more speed.

Hiring, Interviews, and Resumes: Does WPM Matter?

Sometimes. It depends on the role. For positions where typing is a core skill (administrative support, transcription, data entry, chat support), typing speed can be a real hiring factor. For many other roles, employers care more about communication quality and results.

When WPM can help you

  • Admin / assistant roles: fast, accurate typing supports daily workload.
  • Customer support: quick replies improve response time.
  • Data entry / transcription: typing is a direct productivity metric.
  • Student internships: “basic professional readiness” signal.

Should you put typing speed on your resume?

If typing is relevant to the job, yes—but make it credible:

  • Include your speed as: “XX WPM (95%+ accuracy)”
  • Prefer a verified result (take a test right before applying)
  • Don’t exaggerate; accuracy is what employers notice
Want a clean number to list? Take a fresh run on the Typing Speed Test, then maintain it with a short daily routine like the 10-minute typing drill.

What interview tests actually measure

Many typing tests in hiring are less about hitting a huge WPM and more about: staying accurate under pressure. A calm 55 WPM with 97% accuracy often beats a shaky 70 WPM with lots of mistakes. If you want more control, the fundamentals in Typing Tips will help.

How to Improve Typing Speed (Without Burnout)

If you want noticeable progress without making typing practice boring, use a simple weekly structure: short sessions, clear goals, and just enough variety to stay engaged.

Weekly plan (simple and effective)

  • 3 days: 10-minute drill (accuracy + rhythm)
  • 2 days: games or challenges for motivation
  • 1 day: benchmark test + light practice
  • 1 day: rest or “real writing” practice

For the “fun days,” rotate from: Best Free Typing Games.

Best goal for most people

Aim to raise your comfortable speed, not just your peak. A practical goal is improving your average by +2 to +5 WPM over two weeks while keeping accuracy high.

Need help staying consistent? Use: How to Practice Typing Without Getting Bored.

One warning: don’t train bad habits

If you constantly type at a speed where you spam backspace, you may be training panic typing. Slow down slightly until you can maintain accuracy and relaxed rhythm. Speed will come back—and usually jump higher after a week of cleaner practice.

Is Faster Always Better?

Not always. There’s a point of diminishing returns. Going from 30 → 50 WPM often dramatically improves productivity and comfort. Going from 80 → 95 WPM may feel impressive, but it’s a smaller practical gain unless you type all day.

For most jobs, reliable accuracy, comfort, and endurance matter more than extreme speed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is 40 WPM good enough for office work?

Often yes, especially for entry-level roles. But improving to 50–60 WPM usually makes daily tasks feel easier and reduces stress.

Can I get a job with 50 WPM?

Absolutely. Many employers consider 45–55 WPM sufficient, especially with strong accuracy. For typing-heavy roles, higher speeds can be a bonus.

What typing speed do employers look for?

It depends on the role. Admin and support may prefer 50+ WPM. Data entry and transcription often prefer 60+ WPM. Many professional roles care more about accuracy and communication than WPM.

Should I mention my WPM on my resume?

If typing is central to the job (admin, support, data entry, transcription), yes—add your speed with accuracy, like “60 WPM (95%+ accuracy).” If typing isn’t central, it’s optional.

How can I improve quickly without getting bored?

Keep practice short (10–20 minutes), rotate formats (drills + games), and use small weekly goals. Try the 10-minute typing drill and rotate games from Best Free Typing Games.
